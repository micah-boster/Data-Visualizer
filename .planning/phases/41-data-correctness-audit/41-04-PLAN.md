### `.planning/adr/001-z-threshold.md` — Z_THRESHOLD = 2

Context: Anomaly detection uses z-scores to flag batches whose metric values lie far from their cohort norms. The threshold determines how far is "far." Z = 2 corresponds to ~95% confidence under a normal distribution; values beyond ±2 σ are the top/bottom 2.5% on each tail.

Decision: `Z_THRESHOLD = 2` in `src/lib/computation/compute-anomalies.ts:76`.

Alternatives:
- **Z = 3** (~99.7% confidence): too restrictive. Anomaly detection is meant to surface "things worth a human glance" — at z=3 only the most extreme outliers flag and most weeks no anomalies surface, which makes the feature feel broken. Best for high-stakes decisions (deciding whether to fire a partner) — the wrong tradeoff for "should the partnerships lead look at this batch this week?".
- **Z = 1.5** (~87% confidence): too noisy. Surfaces too many flags and erodes signal-to-noise. Daily-driver tools degrade fast under flag fatigue.
- **Two-tail mixture (z = 1.96 × s.d., empirical-Bayes prior)**: more statistically defensible for small cohorts but introduces complexity (priors, hyperparameters) that v4.5's targeted-fixes scope rejects. Architectural rewrite candidate for v5.5 DEBT.

Why z=2: balances signal-to-noise for the partnerships-team daily-driver use case. Round number, easy to reason about ("two standard deviations"), maps to a familiar 95% confidence frame.

Partner overrides: NONE.

When to revisit: if user feedback shows persistent under- or over-flagging after the Phase 41 fixes ship (especially after DCR-07 young-batch censoring removes the systematic false-positive bias).

### `.planning/adr/002-min-groups.md` — MIN_GROUPS = 2

Context: Wave 0 (shipped 2026-04-26) replaced the prior `MIN_FLAGS = 2` gate (which counted individual metric flags) with `MIN_GROUPS = 2` (which counts metric *groups*, where correlated metrics share a group and contribute as a single piece of evidence). Highly-correlated metrics like `COLLECTION_AFTER_3_MONTH` / `_6_MONTH` / `_12_MONTH` were previously triple-counting the same evidence — a partner with a single weak vintage would flag in all three and look like a 3-flag partner when it was really 1 group of evidence.

Decision: `MIN_GROUPS = 2` in `src/lib/computation/compute-anomalies.ts:90`.

Alternatives:
- **MIN_GROUPS = 1**: any single weak group flags the partner. Too noisy — every partner flags on something every week.
- **MIN_GROUPS = 3**: too restrictive. Most partners don't have anomalies in 3 distinct metric groups simultaneously even when they have real problems.
- **Stay with MIN_FLAGS** (pre-Wave-0): rejected because it conflates correlated evidence (the very Wave-0 finding).

Why 2 groups: a single group of correlated metrics flagging is "interesting but maybe just one thing." Two groups means two distinct dimensions of weakness — high-signal that the partner deserves a human look.

Partner overrides: NONE.

When to revisit: if v5.0 triangulation reveals partners flagging that triangulation contradicts (i.e., the partner's own scorecards say they're fine), the metric-group taxonomy or the threshold may need adjustment.

References: also cross-link to `compute-anomalies.ts` `METRIC_GROUPS` dict — the grouping itself is what makes 2 the right number, and any future expansion of `METRIC_GROUPS` may shift the calibration.

### `.planning/adr/003-trending-pct.md` — Trending threshold = 5% relative change

Read `src/lib/computation/compute-trending.ts` for the exact constant name (could be `TRENDING_PCT_THRESHOLD`, `MIN_PCT_DELTA`, etc.). Document:

Decision: 5% relative change between current batch and baseline.

Alternatives:
- **3%**: too noisy.
- **10%**: too restrictive — misses real moves.
- **Absolute (basis points) instead of relative**: rejected because the same absolute move is more meaningful on a 10% baseline than on a 60% baseline (a 5pp change on 10% is huge; on 60% it's modest). Relative captures the "is this a meaningful shift" intuition better.

Why 5%: empirical fit for the partnerships team's "did something move?" gut check.

### `.planning/adr/004-baseline-window.md` — Trending baseline = 3 batches

Decision: 3-batch rolling baseline window.

Alternatives:
- **1-batch**: too volatile — each new batch is compared against the immediately prior one, so trend reads like noise.
- **5+ batches**: too smoothing — slow to react to genuine shifts, and many partners don't have 5+ recent batches to baseline against.
- **Time-windowed (e.g., last 90 days)**: rejected for v4.5 — batch cadences vary across partners, so a fixed time window biases the comparison.

Why 3: enough history to dampen single-batch noise, few enough that newish partners (4+ batches) still get trending. Aligns with "Need 3+ batches for trending" UI message.

Partner overrides: NONE.

When to revisit: if partners with high batch cadence (more frequent than monthly) consistently get noisy trends, OR partners with low cadence (quarterly) consistently lack trends.

DO NOT speculate on values — for ADRs 003 and 004, READ the file `src/lib/computation/compute-trending.ts` first and capture the actual constant names + values present today. If they differ from "5%" or "3 batches" as anticipated by the requirements doc, document the actual values and note the discrepancy.

### `.planning/adr/005-cascade-tiers.md` — KPI cascade breakpoints = 3 / 6 / 12 months

Context: Phase 38 KPI-01 ships a graduated cascade for the KPI cards based on the maximum batch age in scope. The cascade picks WHICH cards to show, not which values to render — a partner with only 4-month batches has no meaningful 12-month rate, so the 12mo card is hidden, not shown empty.

Decision: in `selectCascadeTier(maxBatchAgeMonths)`:
- `< 3` → `['rateSinceInception']`
- `3 ≤ age < 6` → `['rate3mo', 'rateSinceInception']`
- `6 ≤ age < 12` → `['rate3mo', 'rate6mo']`
- `≥ 12` → `['rate6mo', 'rate12mo']`

Code: `src/lib/computation/compute-kpis.ts` `selectCascadeTier`.

Alternatives:
- **Show all 4 cards always (3mo / 6mo / 12mo / since-inception)**: rejected pre-Phase 38 because cards with insufficient batches showed as 0% or "—" and looked broken.
- **Show only the most recently-evaluable card**: rejected — single-card density looks under-built; two cards reads as a proper KPI shelf.
- **Different breakpoints** (e.g., 6 / 12 / 24): rejected — collection-rate horizons are conventionally 3/6/12 in this domain (matches `COLLECTION_AFTER_*_MONTH` columns in Snowflake).

Why these breakpoints: track the canonical horizons in the data. A partner with batches all under 3mo only has the lifetime rate to show; once batches reach 3mo the 3mo card becomes meaningful; once they reach 12mo the 6mo+12mo card pair becomes the meaningful comparison.

Partner overrides: NONE.

Cross-references: `selectCascadeTier` is also smoke-tested in `src/lib/computation/cascade.smoke.ts`.

### `.planning/adr/006-min-placed-denom.md` — MIN_PLACED_DENOMINATOR_DOLLARS = $100K

Context: Wave 0 fix (shipped 2026-04-26). The 3-month KPI rate has a denominator of "placed dollars from batches at least 3mo old." When that denominator is small (e.g., one tiny batch from 4 months ago), the rate is statistically noisy — a $5K-placed batch with 50% collection feels meaningful but it's actually one tail draw on a tiny sample.

Decision: `MIN_PLACED_DENOMINATOR_DOLLARS = 100_000` in `src/lib/computation/compute-kpis.ts:39`. When `placed3mo > 0 && placed3mo < MIN_PLACED_DENOMINATOR_DOLLARS`, the 3mo KPI card is suppressed (rendered with an "Insufficient data" caption) instead of showing a noisy rate.

Code: `src/lib/computation/compute-kpis.ts:142`.

Alternatives:
- **No floor (pre-Wave-0 behavior)**: rejected — produces noisy rates that the partnerships team would either second-guess or trust incorrectly.
- **$50K floor**: rejected — too low; still admits noisy rates.
- **$500K floor**: rejected — too high; suppresses the KPI for many legitimate small partners (e.g., test deals).
- **Fraction-of-total** (e.g., "≥ 5% of partner's lifetime placed"): rejected — variable per partner, harder to reason about, no clean ADR-able number.

Why $100K: empirical fit. Tested against existing partner data — partners with denominators below $100K were the same partners producing the noisy rates that the Wave-0 review surfaced. Round number.

Partner overrides: NONE.

When to revisit: if a partner with a legitimate $80K small-batch program needs the rate visible. The pattern of solution then is per-card "Switch to since-inception" recovery action, not a denominator floor change.

### `.planning/adr/007-penetration-weighting.md` — Dollar-weighted is canonical; account-weighted is column-picker only

Context: Penetration rate is "what fraction of placed accounts (or placed dollars) entered a payment plan." Two natural weightings exist:

- **Dollar-weighted**: `Σ(rate_i × placed_dollars_i) / Σ placed_dollars_i`. Reflects "what fraction of the *value* placed converted." A $1M-placed batch with 10% penetration matters more than a $50K-placed batch with 90% penetration.
- **Account-weighted**: `Σ(rate_i × placed_accounts_i) / Σ placed_accounts_i`. Reflects "what fraction of the *people* placed converted." Diagnostic for digital-channel performance (clicks, opens, etc. correlate with people, not dollars).

Decision: dollar-weighted is the canonical primary metric. It is the value shown in KPI cards, in the default table column, and in the comparison matrix. Account-weighted ships as a secondary column available via the column picker for digital-channel diagnostics.

Code:
- Dollar-weighted compute: `src/lib/columns/root-columns.ts` `weightedByPlaced` (Plan 41-01).
- ColumnDef declaration: `src/lib/columns/definitions.ts` (Plan 41-01) — `aggregation: 'avgWeighted', aggregationWeight: 'TOTAL_AMOUNT_PLACED'`.

Alternatives:
- **Show both as parallel KPI cards**: rejected — KPI shelf density gets noisy fast, and the secondary metric splits user attention. Account-weighted is diagnostic, not headline.
- **Account-weighted as primary**: rejected — aligns with intuition for digital-channel teams but mismatches the dollar-recovery business value the partnerships team optimizes for. The Wave-0 `MIN_PLACED_DENOMINATOR_DOLLARS` decision already canonicalizes dollars as the meaningful denominator.
- **Per-partner choice (toggle in Setup UI)**: rejected — invites apples-and-oranges across partners (one shows dollar-weighted, another shows account-weighted, the cross-partner matrix becomes meaningless). Triangulation requires uniform weighting.

Column labels: "Penetration ($-wt)" for dollar-weighted, "Penetration (acct-wt)" for account-weighted. Both labels make the weighting explicit so a user reading the column header can't misinterpret.

Partner overrides: NONE. Per CONTEXT lock — partner-overrides invite p-hacking and erode triangulation.

References: implementation in `root-columns.ts` (Plan 41-01); aggregation contract in `docs/AGGREGATION-CONTRACT.md`; polarity in `docs/POLARITY-AUDIT.md`.

### `.planning/adr/008-young-batch-censoring.md` — Metric-age-eligibility filter; strict rule

Context: A 4-month-old batch's `COLLECTION_AFTER_12_MONTH` is structurally near-zero — there has been no time for late-stage collection to populate. Comparing it against a norm computed from 24-month-old batches' fully-populated 12mo metrics produces systematic false-positive flags. The DCR-07 fix is to censor young batches on metrics whose horizon they haven't reached.

Decision: **metric-age-eligibility filter** at the anomaly-detection boundary. A batch is eligible for evaluation against a metric only when `batch_age_months >= metric_horizon_months`. Strict rule, no safety margin. Implementation: `src/lib/computation/metric-eligibility.ts` `isMetricEligible`.

UI behavior: ineligible metrics on a young batch render as em-dash with a tooltip explaining "this batch isn't old enough yet." NO anomaly badge, regardless of value. Mirrors the Wave-0 KPI suppression pattern for `MIN_PLACED_DENOMINATOR_DOLLARS`.

Code:
- Helper: `src/lib/computation/metric-eligibility.ts` (Plan 41-02).
- Anomaly callsite: `src/lib/computation/compute-anomalies.ts` (Plan 41-02 patches).
- Synthetic test: `src/lib/computation/young-batch-censoring.test.ts` (Plan 41-02).

Alternatives:
- **Per-age-bucket norms**: compute norms separately for young / mid / old batches. Rejected for v4.5 because it introduces a new dimension to anomaly logic with small-cohort statistical risk (some age buckets will have ≤2 batches at some partners). v5.5 candidate if the eligibility filter proves too coarse.
- **Bayesian / t-distribution rewrite**: out of scope (architectural rewrite). v5.5 DEBT candidate if signal quality remains a complaint after Phase 41 fixes.
- **Soft eligibility (e.g., batch_age >= 0.75 × metric_horizon)**: rejected — adds a tunable parameter without a principled default. Strict rule matches how a human reads the metric.

Why strict: simpler, defensible to partners ("we don't show 12mo metrics on a 4mo batch because there's nothing to show"), no tunable parameter, less surface area than per-age-bucket cohorts.

Partner overrides: NONE. Per-metric eligibility horizons are also NOT configurable — see CONTEXT § Deferred ("Configurable per-metric eligibility horizons rejected for DCR-07").

When to revisit: if metric horizons evolve (e.g., a future `COLLECTION_AFTER_24_MONTH` lands), update `METRIC_HORIZON_MONTHS` in `metric-eligibility.ts`. If the strict rule produces undesirable suppression on edge cases (e.g., a 5.99-month batch on a 6mo metric), discuss whether to add a small grace period — but only with an empirical justification.

References:
- Implementation: Plan 41-02
- Synthetic test: `src/lib/computation/young-batch-censoring.test.ts`
- Wave 0 KPI suppression precedent: ADR 006
